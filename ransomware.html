Ransomware

💻 What is Ransomware?

Ransomware is a type of malicious software that locks or encrypts a victim’s files or entire computer systems. Cybercriminals demand a ransom — often in cryptocurrency — to unlock the files or prevent them from being published online.

Ransomware attacks have grown into a billion-dollar criminal industry, targeting businesses, hospitals, governments, and individuals worldwide.

🔒 How Ransomware Attacks Happen

  • Attackers trick victims into clicking malicious links or opening infected attachments.
  • The ransomware installs silently and encrypts important files.
  • Victims see a ransom note demanding payment to restore access.
  • Sometimes, hackers threaten to leak stolen data publicly if payment isn’t made.

Paying the ransom doesn’t guarantee victims will get their files back — and it fuels more attacks.

🚨 High-Profile Ransomware Cases

  • Colonial Pipeline Attack (2021): Disrupted fuel supply across the U.S. East Coast. The company paid $4.4 million in ransom, partially recovered by the FBI. (Source: FBI, Department of Justice)
  • WannaCry (2017): Infected over 200,000 computers in 150 countries, shutting down hospitals, factories, and businesses. (Source: Europol, NHS UK)
  • REvil Attacks (2020–2021): Targeted companies worldwide, demanding millions in crypto payments and threatening to publish stolen data. (Source: U.S. DOJ, Europol)

These attacks show how ransomware threatens critical infrastructure and global security.

🛡 How to Defend Against Ransomware

  • Keep software and operating systems updated.
  • Use strong antivirus and anti-malware tools.
  • Never click suspicious links or email attachments.
  • Maintain regular backups of important data offline.
  • Limit user permissions so ransomware can’t spread across networks.
  • Develop an incident response plan in case of an attack.
