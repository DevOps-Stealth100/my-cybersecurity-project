Phishing Attacks

🎣 What is Phishing?

Phishing attacks are one of the most common — and dangerous — cybercrimes in the world. In a phishing attack, cybercriminals pose as trusted companies, banks, or government agencies to trick you into revealing personal information like passwords, credit card numbers, or banking details.

They often use emails, text messages, phone calls, or fake websites that look almost identical to the real thing. The goal? Steal your money, your identity, or both.

🕵 How Phishing Works

  • You receive an email or text claiming urgent action is needed (e.g. “Your account will be suspended!”).
  • The message contains a link to a fake website that looks real.
  • You enter your login details or personal info.
  • The attackers capture your data and use it to steal money or commit fraud.

🚨 Real-World Phishing Cases

Phishing isn’t just small-time scams — some of the biggest cyber heists in history started with a simple email:

  • Google & Facebook Scam (2013–2015): Over $100 million stolen when a scammer impersonated a hardware supplier and sent fake invoices. (Source: U.S. Department of Justice)
  • RSA Security Breach (2011): Hackers used phishing emails with malicious Excel files, leading to stolen data about secure tokens used by defense contractors. (Source: RSA)
  • World Health Organization Scam (2020): During COVID-19, scammers sent emails pretending to be WHO, tricking people into clicking malicious links. (Source: Europol and Interpol)

These cases show how even giant corporations and governments can fall victim if individuals click the wrong link.

🛡 How to Protect Yourself from Phishing

  • Never click links in emails or texts unless you’re 100% sure who sent them.
  • Always check the sender’s email address carefully.
  • Look for spelling errors or urgent threats — these are warning signs.
  • Hover over links to see the true web address before clicking.
  • Enable two-factor authentication wherever possible.
  • Report suspicious emails to your email provider, your company IT department, or relevant authorities.
