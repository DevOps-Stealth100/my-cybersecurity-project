DDoS Attacks

üåê What are DDoS Attacks?

A Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) attack floods a website, server, or network with overwhelming amounts of traffic from multiple sources, making it slow, unresponsive, or completely unavailable. Unlike hacking, DDoS attacks aim to disrupt rather than steal data.

These attacks can last minutes, hours, or even days, causing businesses to lose revenue, trust, and critical services.

üöÄ How DDoS Attacks Work

  • Cybercriminals use malware to control thousands of devices (called a ‚Äúbotnet‚Äù).
  • They instruct the botnet to flood a target with massive traffic requests.
  • The server becomes overwhelmed and crashes or slows to a crawl.
  • Attackers sometimes demand ransom to stop the attack.
  • DDoS is also used as a distraction while criminals carry out other attacks.

DDoS attacks are often measured in gigabits or terabits per second of traffic.

üö® Notable DDoS Attack Cases

  • Dyn DNS Attack (2016): A massive DDoS attack targeted Dyn, a major DNS provider, disrupting websites like Twitter, Netflix, and Reddit for hours. (Source: Dyn, Krebs on Security)
  • GitHub Attack (2018): GitHub was hit with the largest DDoS attack recorded at the time, peaking at 1.35 Tbps. Services were briefly disrupted. (Source: GitHub, Akamai)
  • Estonia Attacks (2007): Government and banking websites in Estonia were taken offline for weeks amid political tensions. (Source: NATO CCDCOE)

üõ° How to Stay Safe from DDoS Attacks

  • Businesses should use DDoS protection services from cloud providers or security vendors.
  • Monitor network traffic for unusual spikes in activity.
  • Have incident response plans to handle attacks quickly.
  • Individuals are rarely direct targets, but their devices can become part of botnets if infected with malware.
  • Keep software updated and secure IoT devices to avoid contributing to botnets.
